How does it work?

This cipher is based on enigma machine, developped in Germany in early 20th century by Arthur Sherbius, which was used during World War 2. To use this simplified version:

  • Set the rotors
  • Enter your message (use only a-z characters)
  • Press the Cipher/Decipher button

To Decipher the message:

  • Set the rotors to the same position that was used to encode the message
  • Press the Cipher/Decipher button

The Forgotten History of Enigma

Enigma is a electro-mechanical rotor cipher machine developped and used in Germany in early to mid 20th century. The early models were initially used comercially, but were soon adopted by the military and used by Nazi Germany before and during World War 2.

In 1928 after intercepting a few encoded messages and obtaining the commercial Enigma machine Poles realised that the military version was quite different. They set up a team of young mathematicians including Marian Rajewski, Jerzy Rozycki and Henryk Zygalski. While the British were still using linguists to break code Poles tried the theory of premutations and flaws in German Military message encription procedures. The French were also concerned about by the rise of German militancy were also trying to break the code. They obtained important information from a spy, including two sheets of monthly key settings, but were unable to successfully use the information to decript messages. They decided to pass on the information to the Poles who were able to build replicas of Enigma. In 1938 Germans changed the procedures of enciphering message keys. This meant that new techniques needed to be found to counter this. New techniques like Zygalski sheets or Rajewski's bomba were designed. All this allowed them to read roughly 75% of the intercepted messages.

IN July 1939 the Poles shared with French and British military intelligence representatives their Enigma-decryption techniques and equipment and primised each delegation a Polish-recinstructed Enigma. The dempnstration represented a vital basis for the later British continuation and effort. During the war, British cryptologists, including Alan Turing, decrypted a vast number of messages encripted on Enigma.
